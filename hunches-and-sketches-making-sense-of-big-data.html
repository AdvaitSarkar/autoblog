Hunches and Sketches: Making Sense of Big Data

Abstract

When dealing with massive datasets, traditional visualization methods often fail due to hardware limitations. This article explores how approximate visualizations can serve as “sketches” of large datasets, allowing analysts to rapidly form “hunches” about patterns and relationships without processing the entire dataset. This approach combines data summarization techniques with uncertainty visualization to support faster, more interactive data exploration.

Big Data Visualizations Are Breaking Our Computers

Picture yourself trying to make sense of 10 million data points. You open your trusty Excel spreadsheet or fire up R on your laptop, hoping to create a simple scatterplot. Then you wait. And wait. And wait some more.

This is the reality of working with big data on everyday computers. What should be a quick visual check becomes an exercise in patience, completely undermining the purpose of visualization: rapid insight.

As data volumes continue to grow exponentially, even advances in computing power struggle to keep pace. More powerful hardware and distributed computing systems paradoxically contribute to the problem by enabling the collection of even larger datasets. It’s a never-ending cycle that keeps visualizations just out of reach for analysts working with standard equipment.

The Power of Approximation Can Break This Cycle

What if you didn’t need to visualize every single data point to understand the story your data is telling?

This is where approximation techniques enter the picture. Rather than rendering all 10 million points, these methods create representative samples or summaries that maintain the essential patterns while drastically reducing processing requirements. The difference is dramatic: what might take minutes or hours to render can instead appear almost instantly.

Various approximation techniques have emerged in recent decades. Some use random sampling to select a subset of data points. Others employ “sketches” — not visual sketches, but rather specialized data structures and algorithms that compress information while preserving key properties. The Bloom filter, for example, allows for fast checking of whether an element might be in a set, using far less memory than storing the complete set.

These techniques trade perfect accuracy for speed and interactivity, introducing small, quantifiable errors in exchange for making the visualization possible at all.

Good Visualizations Tell Us What We Need to Know, Not Everything There Is to Know

Consider what you’re actually looking for when examining a visualization.

When looking at a scatterplot of customer data, you might not need to know the exact coordinates of each point. You’re likely more interested in whether there’s a linear relationship between variables, or if there are distinct clusters forming. A histogram doesn’t need to show the precise frequency count for each bin; the overall shape of the distribution often provides the insight you need.

The paper distinguishes between two approaches to data analysis. The “top-down” approach starts with specific hypotheses and tests them statistically. The “bottom-up” approach is more exploratory, helping analysts determine which questions are worth asking in the first place. Experienced analysts typically blend these approaches, using visualizations to quickly identify promising hypotheses before conducting more rigorous statistical tests.

Approximate visualizations excel at supporting this exploratory phase. They allow analysts to rapidly form “hunches” — informal hypotheses about patterns in the data that can guide further investigation.

Sketchy Visualizations Help Us Remember They’re Just Approximations

When working with approximations, it’s crucial to communicate the inherent uncertainty. This is where visualization techniques for uncertainty come into play.

The most familiar example might be error bars on charts, but the repertoire extends much further. Transparency, blurring, animation, and even deliberately sketch-like renderings can all signal that a visualization represents an approximation rather than exact data.

These visual cues serve an important cognitive function. By appearing informal or incomplete, sketch-like visualizations encourage interaction and questioning. They remind viewers that they’re looking at approximations designed to spark ideas rather than deliver final answers.

For example, an approximate scatterplot might show a general trend line without claiming precise regression coefficients. An approximate histogram might reveal a bimodal distribution without specifying exact frequencies. These visualizations aren’t meant to replace statistical analysis; they’re meant to guide it.

Combining Fast Approximation with Uncertainty Visualization Creates a New Kind of Data Exploration Tool

The potential for combining approximation techniques with uncertainty visualization extends beyond simply making big data more manageable. This combination creates a new paradigm for interactive data exploration.

Traditional data analysis tools often force analysts to choose between speed and accuracy. They can either work with a small subset of data that processes quickly or wait for complete but slow results from the full dataset. Approximate visualizations offer a middle path, providing immediate feedback that guides analysts toward promising areas for deeper investigation.

This approach aligns with calls for increased integration between statistical techniques and information visualization. It acknowledges that human perception and machine computation each have distinct strengths in the analytical process. By leveraging fast approximation algorithms and deliberately sketch-like visualizations, these tools can support rapid, interactive exploration of large datasets on ordinary computers.

Such tools would enable analysts to quickly form and test informal hypotheses, or “hunches,” about their data. These hunches might include questions like: Is this pattern signal or noise? Does the data contain clusters? What’s the general shape of this distribution? With traditional tools, answering these questions for large datasets would be prohibitively time-consuming.

The Future of Data Analysis Might Look More Like Sketching Than Computing

The vision proposed represents a shift in how we think about data analysis tools. Rather than treating approximation as a necessary evil, it embraces approximation as a feature that enables new kinds of interaction with data.

This approach has particular relevance for analysts working with commodity hardware rather than specialized high-performance computing environments. It democratizes big data analysis, making it accessible to a broader range of practitioners.

The road ahead involves identifying common types of visual insights that analysts seek and determining which approximation techniques best preserve those insights. It also requires developing effective ways to visualize transformations with probabilistic error bounds, communicating uncertainty without undermining confidence in the general patterns being revealed.

By combining fast approximation techniques with thoughtful visualization of uncertainty, we can create tools that support the inherently iterative, exploratory nature of data analysis. These tools won’t replace rigorous statistical methods, but they will help analysts navigate the infinite space of possible questions to find those worth asking.

References

Proceedings of the 8th International Conference on the Theory and Application of Diagrams, Graduate Symposium, 2014 (DIAGRAMS 2014) (pp. 18-22).
