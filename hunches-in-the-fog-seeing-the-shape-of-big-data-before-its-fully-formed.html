Hunches in the Fog: Seeing the Shape of Big Data Before It’s Fully Formed

Abstract: This article breaks down the paper Hunches and Sketches: Rapid Interactive Exploration of Large Datasets through Approximate Visualisations, which explores how sampling and probabilistic algorithms can make data visualisation faster and more usable. It introduces a framework where imperfect, sketchy visualisations help analysts form rough hypotheses—“hunches”—before digging deeper. This post explains how these methods help tame the complexity of big data, even on basic hardware.

Full citation: Sarkar, A., Blackwell, A. F., Jamnik, M., & Spott, M. (2014). Hunches and Sketches: Rapid Interactive Exploration of Large Datasets through Approximate Visualisations. Proceedings of the Diagrams 2014 Graduate Symposium.

The speed of thought is faster than your graphics card

There’s a moment every data analyst dreads. The dataset is loaded. The question is forming. But when the “plot” button is clicked, everything slows to a crawl. Not just the computer, but the brain. It’s like trying to dance through molasses.

This isn’t a glitch. It’s a growing problem. As the paper puts it, “a scatterplot of 10,000 data points renders relatively quickly… However, as of this writing it is grindingly slow to render a scatterplot of 10,000,000 points.” The visualisation tools we rely on for fast, intuitive pattern recognition buckle under the weight of modern data volumes. No amount of caffeine—or RAM—can keep up.

The rise in data size has outpaced our ability to interact with it visually in real time. Commodity laptops can no longer be expected to keep pace. And while distributed computing promises more power, it also helps generate even more data. It’s a treadmill with no off switch.

Fast, imperfect visuals beat perfect, slow ones when exploring

Enter the paper’s central idea: instead of fighting the mountain of data, sketch it. Not artistically, but computationally.

This isn’t sketching as in charcoal and paper. In data science, a “sketch” is a kind of algorithmic shorthand—a way to compress large datasets into quick, approximate representations. Techniques like Bloom filters or linear counting algorithms let systems estimate, rather than enumerate.

But the trick is not just in using these sketches computationally. It’s in seeing them. The paper argues that “data summarisation techniques can be used to interactively render approximate, exploratory visualisations.” Think of it like squinting at a blurry image to catch the shape of it, rather than waiting for the whole thing to render pixel-perfect.

This means visualisations don’t need to be accurate to the last decimal. They need to be fast enough to support exploration. The researchers show that these “sketchy” visuals can actually help people think better, because they support rapid, informal reasoning. They help form what the paper calls “hunches.”

A hunch is worth a thousand queries

A hunch is what happens before the formal analysis. It’s that flicker of “this looks interesting” that sparks a deeper dive. The paper explains: “Visualisations of uncertainty emphasise that these summaries will not support exact inference, but instead facilitate rapid informal reasoning and the formation of ‘hunches.’”

This flips the usual script. Traditional analysis says: ask a precise question, then answer it rigorously. This approach says: wander through the data with loose eyes and fast tools. Let your hunches bloom, then pick the ones worth pursuing.

The idea is to support “hunch-driven reasoning,” which gives informal answers to open-ended questions like: Is this signal or noise? Are there clusters here? These early impressions can then be sharpened with formal tools.

And crucially, these hunches are not pulled out of thin air. They’re visually grounded, albeit imperfectly. “The reduced dataset is sufficient for the analyst to form the hunch… that a linear model may be viable,” the authors explain, even if the trend line is only approximately right.

Sketchiness is a feature, not a bug

The roughness of these visualisations is not a flaw. It’s part of their power. When a chart looks sketchy—blurred, translucent, less precise—it signals that the data behind it is approximate. This encourages a different kind of engagement.

“Informal, sketch-like visualisations are thought to influence willingness to interact with and question the visualisation,” the paper notes. They invite interpretation, rather than suggest authority.

In a way, these visualisations are like whiteboard drawings: quick, dirty, and conversational. They say, “Here’s an idea—what do you think?” rather than “Here is the truth—trust me.” This can be freeing, especially for analysts navigating unfamiliar terrain.

This is more than an aesthetic choice. It connects to deeper ideas in design and cognition. “Sketches are not simply degraded versions of a canonically accurate visual representation,” the paper points out. They serve specific cognitive and social functions—like inviting feedback, sparking ideas, and avoiding premature commitment to a conclusion.

Visualising uncertainty is better than pretending it isn’t there

Every visualisation contains uncertainty. The difference is whether it’s acknowledged. The authors argue for bringing that uncertainty into the open, not hiding it under precise-looking graphs.

Techniques like error bars are old hat, but the paper nods to newer methods too—transparency, blurring, painterly rendering, even animation. These aren’t just artsy embellishments. They encode the idea that what’s being shown is an approximation. It’s a crucial cue for viewers.

The point is not to reject precision, but to sequence it wisely. Fast, sketchy visuals help identify promising questions. Detailed analysis comes after. Think of it like surveying a forest from above before deciding where to hike.

The future of analysis is interactive, uncertain, and fast

Taken together, these ideas propose a new kind of analysis workflow. One where approximate computation meets visual exploration. Where insights are not plucked from perfect plots, but shaped in the fog of imperfect ones.

This vision includes “intelligent discovery assistants”—tools that collaborate with users to produce and refine hunches. These wouldn’t just show the data, but help interpret it. They’d operate quickly, run on everyday machines, and foreground uncertainty instead of pretending it doesn’t exist.

Importantly, the paper resists overpromising. “It is important to consider how we might visualise transformations that make no such guarantees,” it states, acknowledging the risks of relying on imperfect summaries. But the authors argue that even flawed approximations can be useful—if they’re framed correctly.

The message is clear: speed matters, uncertainty is real, and the shape of data often appears in the sketch before the sculpture.
